What it is

A community radio network for Guam

Meshtastic is open-source LoRa mesh radio — small, cheap devices that relay text messages and GPS positions across long distances without internet or cell infrastructure. Mesh671 is Guam’s local deployment, run by the community, for the community.

📡
Mesh networking
Every node is also a relay. A message hops from node to node until it arrives — the more nodes on the island, the stronger and farther the network reaches.
📍
GPS tracking
Share your location on a private map with friends or family. Great for boating, hiking Guam’s trails, or keeping track of where your group is scattered.
🔒
Private channels
Set up a private channel with a passphrase for your household or crew. AES-128 encrypted. The public Mesh671 channel is open for island-wide conversation.
⚡
Low power
A USB power bank keeps a node running for days. A small solar panel keeps it alive indefinitely. A node on your roof costs almost nothing to run.
📻
Ham radio friendly
915 MHz ISM band — no license needed to participate. Licensed hams can run higher-power backbone nodes that help build the network’s spine.
🌏
Works when it counts
Cell networks go down in typhoons. This doesn’t — there’s nothing central to fail. It’s not built for emergencies specifically, but it’s good to have when they happen.
Get on the mesh

Three steps to your first node

Works on iPhone or Android. DIY kits need a laptop for the initial flash — after that everything runs from your phone.

01
Get a node and power it up
Any Meshtastic-compatible hardware works. See the gear section for what we recommend for Guam. For DIY kits, open the Meshtastic web flasher in Chrome or Edge, pick your board, and flash the firmware — takes about 5 minutes. Ready-to-go devices like the T1000-E just need power.
flasher.meshtastic.org
02
Install the app and pair your node
Download the free Meshtastic app on iOS or Android. Tap the + button and pair with your node over Bluetooth. Give your node a name — callsign, handle, whatever you want others on the mesh to see.
iOS App StoreGoogle Play
03
Set your region and join Mesh671
In the app go to Radio Config → LoRa → Region and select US_915. Then go to Channels and add the Mesh671 public channel. You’re on the island mesh.
Region: US_915Channel: Mesh671
+
Want a private channel for your group?
In Channels, add a new channel with a name and passphrase you share with your people. Anyone with the same channel name and PSK can communicate privately, encrypted end-to-end. Share it as a QR code from the app for easy setup on family phones.
Internet bridge

Connect beyond radio range with MQTT

MQTT lets a Wi-Fi or phone-connected node bridge your local LoRa mesh to the internet, connecting nodes across Guam or across the Pacific that can’t hear each other over radio.

01
You need a gateway node or your phone
ESP32-based nodes (T-Beam, Heltec) can connect directly to Wi-Fi and act as a gateway for the whole local mesh. If you have an nRF52840 kit like the XIAO, use your phone as a proxy instead — enable Client Proxy in MQTT settings and keep Bluetooth connected.
02
Enable MQTT and configure the broker
Go to Module Config → MQTT and toggle it on. Use these settings to join the Guam-specific topic on the public Meshtastic broker:

Using msh/GU keeps Guam traffic grouped on the broker. The default msh/US also works and connects you to mainland nodes — useful if family stateside runs Meshtastic too.

03
Enable uplink and downlink on your channel
In Channels, open Mesh671 or LongFast and enable both Uplink and Downlink. Save and reboot. Your node will push local radio traffic to the broker and pull remote traffic back down to your local mesh.
Uplink: onDownlink: on
!
A note on encryption over MQTT
With Encryption enabled, your channel’s key is preserved over MQTT. The default LongFast channel uses a publicly known key — treat it as an open channel. For private comms use a channel with your own PSK. Position data sent via Map Reporting is always unencrypted so the public map can display it.

Community

The mesh grows when you join it

No company owns Mesh671. No one can shut it down. Every node someone puts up strengthens something that will outlast any individual piece of hardware. The Guam Amateur Radio Club keeps it coordinated — but the network itself belongs to the island.

Guam Amateur Radio Club
Monthly meetings where you can get hands-on help with your node, see live demos, and connect with other operators. No license required to participate — all are welcome.
